\section{Introduction}

In this Thermal Systems project, we set out to design a fin bank for a given duct,  create a model describing the system as a whole, and analyze how changing different variables within the model affects the system. The geometry of the duct itself was given to us. To simplify the task, this project was split into three parts, described below.

\subsection{Part 1 - Fin Bank}
\subsubsection{Introduction to Part 1}

The first part of this project focused on developing the model used to describe convective heat transfer within the fin bank. While there were many ways to model the system (dedicated iterative solver software, Python, etc.) we chose to use MATLAB because of its familiarity and ease setup among teammates with the required software. 

Part 1 was the most straightforward of the project, simply consisting of calculating various parameters required for describing the fin bank. This included fin efficiencies, fin bank areas, and thermal resistances. After creating this model, we verified it by using it to solve Example 2.11(a) from Slide 79 of the Thermal Conduction notes, previously solved in class by Dr. Miller. This script, along with all other MATLAB scripts used throughout the project, can be found in Figures XXX-XXX in the appendix. %%FIXME

\subsubsection{Governing Equations and Modeling Methodology}

The given fin bank is modeled using one-dimensional, steady-state heat transfer combined with the thermal resistance method. This approach follows the same methodology used in Example 2.11 from the course notes, where heat transfer is modeled analogous to electrical current flow through resistors.

Under these assumptions, the total heat transfer rate is expressed as

\begin{equation}
\dot{Q} = \frac{T_b - T_{\infty}}{R_{\text{tot}}}
\end{equation}

where $T_b$ is the duct base temperature, $T_{\infty}$ is the bulk air temperature, and $R_{\text{tot}}$ is the total thermal resistance of the fin bank.

\vspace{0.5em}

The modeling procedure follows a structured five-step process:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Determine finned and unfinned surface areas
    \item Compute fin efficiency
    \item Compute overall fin efficiency
    \item Construct the thermal resistance network
    \item Solve for heat transfer rate and system performance
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Part 2 - Cooling System}
\subsubsection{Introduction to Part 2}

The second part of this project focused on modifying our model to account for the cooling system, i.e. the air flow through the duct from a cooling fan. This included drawing a diagram of the "ducts" between each fin, which we treated as an enclosed duct. Additionally, this part of the project was the most computationally complex, as we needed to compute the hydraulic diameter and Reynolds number for the flow between fins, estimate the bulk temperature and mean air velocity in the duct, the convective heat transfer coefficient of the flow between fins, and the fan power required to cool the system.

\subsubsection{Cooling Flow Model and Convective Heat Transfer}

In Part 2, the fin-bank model is extended to include forced convection through the duct. Unlike Part 1, the convection coefficient $h$ is no longer prescribed but instead determined from internal flow correlations. This introduces coupling between the thermal and fluid systems, requiring an iterative solution.

\subsubsection{Surrogate Duct and Flow Assumptions}

The flow between fins is modeled as a series of identical rectangular ducts (also referred to as surrogate ducts), formed by the spacing between adjacent fins. The flow is assumed to be the following:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Steady-state
    \item Fully developed and turbulent
    \item One-dimensional in the axial direction
\end{itemize}

Under these assumptions, bulk properties of the flow can be related using conservation laws.

\subsubsection{Coupled Thermal–Fluid Solution}

The fin bank resistance depends on the convection coefficient $h$, while $h$ depends on the Reynolds number, which depends on flow velocity and thus on $\dot{m}_{\text{air}}$. Meanwhile, $\dot{m}_{\text{air}}$ is determined from the energy balance, which depends on the total heat transfer rate.

This creates a nonlinear coupling between:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Convection coefficient $h$
    \item Reynolds number $Re_D$
    \item Mass flow rate $\dot{m}_{\text{air}}$
    \item Air temperature $T_{\infty}$
\end{itemize}

To resolve this, an iterative numerical method is employed. A trial value of $h$ is assumed, and the corresponding flow conditions and temperatures are computed. The resulting air temperature is compared to that predicted by the thermal resistance model, and the value of $h$ is adjusted until convergence is achieved.

\subsubsection{Iterative Solution Procedure}

The convection coefficient $h$ is implicitly coupled to the flow and thermal models through the Reynolds number, Nusselt equation, and energy balance. As a result, $h$ cannot be solved analytically and must be determined numerically.

A root-finding approach is used to enforce consistency between the fluid and thermal models. Specifically, the solution is obtained by satisfying

\begin{equation}
f(h) = T_{\infty,\text{air}} - T_{\infty,\text{fin}} = 0
\end{equation}

where $T_{\infty,\text{air}}$ is computed from the energy balance and $T_{\infty,\text{fin}}$ is obtained from the thermal resistance network.

An iterative search is performed by incrementally adjusting $h$ until the sign of $f(h)$ changes, followed by successive reduction of the step size to refine the solution. This process continues until the difference between the two temperature estimates falls below a prescribed tolerance.

At each iteration, the Reynolds number, velocity, mass flow rate, and temperatures are updated, ensuring a self-consistent solution for the coupled thermal–fluid system.

\subsection{Part 3 - Performance Analysis}
\subsubsection{Introduction to Part 3}

The third and final part of this project sought to use the models developed in the first two parts and evaluate their performances as a function of various design parameters for the fin bank (number of fins, fin thickness, fin length, etc.). The problem statement provided a defined fin bank geometry, which we were able to use to analyze the model's performance.  Additionally, the performance was computed used a given benchmark CPU performance score given by the problem statement.

\subsubsection{Performance Analysis and Parametric Study}

In Part 3, the validated thermal–fluid model is used to evaluate system performance as a function of key fin bank design parameters. The objective is to maximize the performance metric defined as the ratio of computational performance to total power consumption.

\subsection{Part 1 - Fin Bank}
\subsubsection{Fin Geometry and Surface Areas}

The total heat transfer area consists of contributions from both the fins and the unfinned duct surface. These are defined as

\begin{equation}
A_b = wt
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
A_{\text{fin}} = 2wL_c
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
A_{\text{fins}} = N A_{\text{fin}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
A_{\text{unfin}} = A_{\text{nofins}} - N A_b
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
A_{\text{tot}} = A_{\text{fins}} + A_{\text{unfin}}
\end{equation}

where $N$ is the total number of fins, $w$ is the fin width, and $L_c$ is the corrected fin length.

\subsubsection{Fin Efficiency}

Each fin is modeled as a straight rectangular fin with convection along its surface and at the tip. The fin efficiency is given by %FIXME there is no convection at the tip

\begin{equation}
m = \sqrt{\frac{2h}{kt}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
L_c = L + \frac{t}{2}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\eta_{\text{fin}} = \frac{\tanh(mL_c)}{mL_c}
\end{equation}

This formulation accounts for conduction along the fin and convection from its surface, consistent with standard fin analysis derived from an energy balance on a differential element.

\subsubsection{Overall Fin Efficiency}

Because the fin bank consists of both finned and unfinned surfaces, an overall efficiency is defined as

\begin{equation}
\eta_{\text{tot}} = 1 - \frac{A_{\text{fins}}}{A_{\text{tot}}}(1 - \eta_{\text{fin}})
\end{equation}

This term accounts for the reduced effectiveness of the finned region relative to an ideal isothermal surface. %%FIXME don't talk about overall efficiency, just how we used efficiency (below)

\subsubsection{Thermal Resistance Network}

Heat is transferred from the base through two parallel paths: the finned surfaces and the unfinned duct surface. Using the thermal resistance analogy, the convection resistances are

\begin{equation}
R_{\text{fins}} = \frac{1}{h A_{\text{fins}} \eta_{\text{fin}}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
R_{\text{unfin}} = \frac{1}{h A_{\text{unfin}}}
\end{equation}

These resistances act in parallel, giving the total resistance

\begin{equation}
R_{\text{tot}} = \left( \frac{1}{R_{\text{fins}}} + \frac{1}{R_{\text{unfin}}} \right)^{-1}
\end{equation}

This formulation reflects the physical reality that heat simultaneously transfers through both regions, analogous to current splitting across parallel resistors.

\subsubsection{Heat Transfer Rate and CPU Power}

Using the total resistance, the total heat dissipation from the fin bank is

\begin{equation}
\dot{Q}_{\text{tot}} = \frac{T_b - T_{\infty}}{R_{\text{tot}}}
\end{equation}

The allowable heat dissipation per CPU is then

\begin{equation}
\dot{Q}_{\text{CPU}} = \frac{\dot{Q}_{\text{tot}}}{N_{\text{CPU}}}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Airflow Requirements (Energy Balance)}

To determine the required airflow, a steady-flow energy balance (First Law of Thermodynamics) is applied to the air passing through the duct:

\begin{equation}
\dot{Q}_{\text{tot}} = \dot{m}_{\text{air}} c_p \Delta T_{\text{air}}
\end{equation}

Solving for the mass flow rate gives

\begin{equation}
\dot{m}_{\text{air}} = \frac{\dot{Q}_{\text{tot}}}{c_p \Delta T_{\text{air}}}
\end{equation}

This relation assumes constant specific heat and negligible kinetic and potential energy changes.

\subsection{Part 2 - Cooling System}

\subsubsection{Reynolds Number and Mean Velocity}

The Reynolds number for the surrogate duct is defined as

\begin{equation}
Re_D = \frac{u_m D_h}{\nu}
\end{equation}

where $u_m$ is the mean velocity, $D_h$ is the hydraulic diameter, and $\nu$ is the kinematic viscosity.

Rearranging gives the mean velocity:

\begin{equation}
u_m = \frac{Re_D \nu}{D_h}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Nusselt Number and Convection Coefficient}

Assuming turbulent flow, the Nusselt number equation is therefore used:

\begin{equation}
Nu = 0.023 Re_D^{0.8} Pr^{0.4}
\end{equation}

The convection heat transfer coefficient is then

\begin{equation}
h = \frac{Nu \, k_{\text{air}}}{D_h}
\end{equation}

Combining these relations yields an implicit dependence between $h$ and $Re_D$, requiring an iterative solution. %%FIXME not exactly true

\subsubsection{Mass Flow Rate (Conservation of Mass)}

Applying conservation of mass to the duct: %%FXIME incomplete sentence

\begin{equation}
\dot{m}_{\text{air}} = \rho A_c u_m
\end{equation}

where $A_c$ is the cross-sectional flow area and $\rho$ is the air density.

\subsubsection{Energy Balance on the Air (First Law)}

Applying the First Law of Thermodynamics to the control volume of air flowing through the duct:

\begin{equation}
\dot{Q}_{\text{tot}} = \dot{m}_{\text{air}} c_p (T_{me} - T_{mi})
\end{equation}

which gives the exit (mean) air temperature:

\begin{equation}
T_{me} = T_{mi} + \frac{\dot{Q}_{\text{tot}}}{\dot{m}_{\text{air}} c_p}
\end{equation}

An average bulk air temperature is approximated as

\begin{equation}
T_{\infty} \approx \frac{T_{mi} + T_{me}}{2}
\end{equation}

This temperature is used in the fin convection model.

\subsubsection{Thermal Resistance with Conduction and Contact Effects}

In addition to convection from the fin bank, conduction through the duct wall and thermal contact resistance between CPUs and the duct are included:

\begin{equation}
R_{\text{paste}} = \frac{1}{h_c A_{\text{CPUs}}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
R_{\text{cond}} = \frac{t_{\text{duct}}}{k_{\text{duct}} A_{\text{walls}}}
\end{equation}

These are combined in series with the fin bank resistance:

\begin{equation}
R_{\text{total, system}} = R_{\text{paste}} + R_{\text{cond}} + R_{\text{fin bank}}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Fan Performance and Power Requirement}

Once the required volumetric flow rate is determined,

\begin{equation}
Q_{\text{fan}} = \frac{\dot{m}_{\text{air}}}{\rho}
\end{equation}

the fan power is computed using the empirical relation provided in the problem statement:

\begin{equation}
P_{\text{fan}} = 10{,}000 \left(\frac{Q_{\text{fan}}}{100{,}000}\right)^3
\end{equation}

This allows direct coupling between thermal requirements and system power consumption.

\subsection{Part 3 - Performance Analysis}

\subsubsection{Performance Metric}

The performance of the system is quantified as

\begin{equation}
\text{Performance} = \frac{CPU_{\text{perf, total}}}{P_{\text{tot}}}
\end{equation}

where the total power is given by

\begin{equation}
P_{\text{tot}} = P_{\text{CPUs}} + P_{\text{fan}}
\end{equation}

The CPU performance is related to the power of a single CPU using the empirical relation

\begin{equation}
CPU_{\text{perf}} = 39000\left(1 - e^{-0.02P_{CPU}}\right)
\end{equation}

which is inverted to determine the required CPU power for a specified performance level.

The function perf() evaluates the system performance for a given fin geometry and operating condition by solving the coupled thermal and fluid models. It internally determines the convection coefficient through an iterative procedure, computes the required airflow and fan power, and returns the ratio of CPU performance to total system power.

\subsubsection{Parametric Analysis}

A parametric study is conducted by varying one design parameter at a time while holding all others constant at the baseline geometry. For each configuration, the coupled thermal–fluid model from Parts 1 and 2 is solved to determine the required airflow, convection coefficient, and fan power.

The following parameters are evaluated:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fin length ($L$):} affects total surface area and fin efficiency
    \item \textbf{Number of fins ($N$):} influences both heat transfer area and flow restriction
    \item \textbf{Fin thickness ($t$):} impacts conduction within the fin and spacing between fins
    \item \textbf{Material conductivity ($k$):} affects fin efficiency and overall thermal resistance
\end{itemize}

For each case, the performance metric is computed and plotted as a function of the varied parameter.

\subsubsection{Two-Parameter Analysis}

In addition to single-parameter sweeps, a two-dimensional analysis is performed by simultaneously varying the number of fins and the CPU power. A surface plot is generated to illustrate the dependence of system performance on these variables:

\begin{equation}
\text{Performance} = f(N, P_{CPU})
\end{equation}

This analysis provides insight into how optimal fin design varies with operating conditions.

function [h, Re_D, T_me] = findh2(P_CPUs, h_c, t_DUCT, A_c, ...
    D_h, T_mi, c_p, k_air, k_duct, A_c_walls, A_c_CPUs, N, ...
    t, L, L_SIDE, L_DUCT, w, rho, nu, Pr)
%convection heat transfer coefficient, W/(m^2*K) CHECK UNITS
    it = 1;
    precision = 0.00001;
    h = 1;
    while it >= precision
        [new, Re_D, T_me] = tinf(P_CPUs, h, h_c, t_DUCT, ...
            A_c, D_h, T_mi, c_p, k_air, k_duct, A_c_walls, ...
            A_c_CPUs, N, t, L, L_SIDE, L_DUCT, w, rho, nu, Pr);
        while new >= 0
            [new, Re_D, T_me] = tinf(P_CPUs, h, h_c, t_DUCT, ...
                A_c, D_h, T_mi, c_p, k_air, k_duct, ...
                A_c_walls, A_c_CPUs, N, t, L, L_SIDE, ...
                L_DUCT, w, rho, nu, Pr);
            h = h + it;
        end
        h = h - 2 * it;
        it = it/10;
    end
end

\end{verbatim}

function [perf_out, P_fan, Re_D, T_me] = perf(CPU_perf, P_CPUs, ...
    h_c, t_DUCT, T_mi, c_p, k_air, k_duct, A_c_walls, ...
    A_c_CPUs, N, t, L, L_SIDE, L_DUCT, w, rho, nu, Pr)
    s = (L_SIDE-L-N/4*t)/(N/4+1);        %Spacing between fins, meters
    A_c = L_SIDE^2 - L*t*N;              %Cross sectional area, meters^2
    D_h = (2*L*s)/(L+s); %Hydraulic diameter of surragate duct, meters
    [h, Re_D, T_me] = findh2(P_CPUs, h_c, t_DUCT, A_c, D_h, ...
        T_mi, c_p, k_air, k_duct, A_c_walls, A_c_CPUs, N, ...
        t, L, L_SIDE, L_DUCT, w, rho, nu, Pr)

    %% Calculate Mean Velocity
    %assume conservation of mass to use:
    %Reynolds number for surrogate duct:
    u_m = Re_D * nu / D_h
    
    %% Calculate mass flow rate
    %given T_me = T_mi + Q_dot/(m_dot * c_p)
    m_dot_air = rho * A_c * u_m; %kg/s %Q_dot_tot/(c_p*(T_me-T_mi))
    %commented out is the necessary m_dot - requires iterative solution
    
    %Q_fan conversion
    Q_fan = (m_dot_air / rho) * 60 * 1000;   %In L/min
    
    P_fan = ((Q_fan/100000)^3)*10000;

    perf_out = CPU_perf/(P_CPUs+P_fan);
end
\end{verbatim}
\subsubsection{tinf.m}
\begin{verbatim}
function [diff, Re_D, T_me] = tinf(P_CPUs, h, h_c, t_DUCT, ...
    A_c, D_h, T_mi, c_p, k_air, k_duct, A_c_walls, A_c_CPUs, ...
    N, t, L, L_SIDE, L_DUCT, w, rho, nu, Pr)
    Q_dot_tot = P_CPUs;
    
    %% Calculate Reynolds Number
    %Nusselt Number Eqn:
    Re_D = ((h*D_h)/(k_air*0.023*Pr^0.4))^(1/0.8); %Reynolds number for the duct
    
    %% Calculate Mean Velocity
    %assume conservation of mass to use:
    %Reynolds number for surrogate duct:
    u_m = Re_D * nu / D_h;
    
    %% Calculate mass flow rate
    %given T_me = T_mi + Q_dot/(m_dot * c_p)
    m_dot_air = rho * A_c * u_m; %kg/s %Q_dot_tot/(c_p*(T_me-T_mi))
    %commented out is the necessary m_dot - requires iterative solution
    
    T_inf_air = (Q_dot_tot/(m_dot_air*c_p) + 2 * T_mi)/2;
    
    %% Calculate Thermal Resistances
    R_paste = 1/(h_c * A_c_CPUs);            
    R_cond = t_DUCT/(k_duct * A_c_walls);
    
    %% Calculate Fin Efficiency
    m = sqrt((2 * h) / (k_duct * t));    %constant for calculating efficiency
    L_c = L + t / 2;                     %corrected fin length, meters
    eta_fin = tanh(m * L_c) / (m * L_c); %single fin efficiency
    
    %% Calculate Areas
    A_b = w * t;                         %area of single fin base, meters^2
    A_fin = 2 * w * L_c;                 %area of single fin, meters^2
    A_fins = N * A_fin;                  %area of all of the fins, meters^2
    A_nofins = L_SIDE * L_DUCT * 4;      %area with fins removed, meters^2
    A_unfin = A_nofins - N * A_b; %area of fin bank with no fins, meters^2
    A_tot = A_fins + A_unfin;            %total area of fin bank, meters^2
    %eta_tot = 1 - ((A_fins) / (A_tot)) * (1 - eta_fin);
    %unused total efficiency
    
    %% Calculate Fin Convection Resistance
    %R_tot = 1 / (h * A_tot * eta_tot) 
    %fins convection thermal resistance, K/W
    R_fins = 1 / (h * A_fins * eta_fin);
    R_unfin = 1 / (h * A_unfin); %unfin convection thermal resistance, K/W
    %total resistance - parallel fin and unfin resistors, K/W
    R_tot = 1/(1/R_fins+1/R_unfin);
    
    T_inf_fin = 90 - Q_dot_tot * (R_paste + R_cond + R_tot);
    
    diff = T_inf_air - T_inf_fin;

    T_me = Q_dot_tot/(m_dot_air * c_p) +T_mi;
end
\end{verbatim}
