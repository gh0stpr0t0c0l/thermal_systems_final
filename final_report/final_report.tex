\section{Introduction}

In this Thermal Systems project, we set out to design a fin bank for a given duct,  create a model describing the system as a whole, and analyze how changing different variables within the model affects the system. The geometry of the duct itself was given to us, and remains unmodified until the end of the project. To simplify the task, this project was split into three parts, described below.

\subsection{Part 1 - Fin Bank}
\subsubsection{Introduction to Part 1}

The first part of this project focused on developing the model used to describe convective heat transfer within the fin bank. While there were many ways to model the system (dedicated iterative solver software, Python, etc.) we chose to use MATLAB because of its familiarity and ease setup among teammates with the required software. 

Part 1 was the most straightforward of the project, simply consisting of calculating various parameters required for describing the fin bank. This included fin efficiencies, fin bank areas, and thermal resistances. After creating this model, we verified it by using it to solve Example 2.11(a) from Slide 79 of the Thermal Conduction notes, previously solved in class by Dr. Miller. This script, along with all other MATLAB scripts used throughout the project, can be found in the appendix. %%FIXME

\subsubsection{Thermal Resistance Equation and Modeling Methodology}

The given fin bank is modeled using one-dimensional, steady-state heat transfer combined with the thermal resistance method. This approach follows the same methodology used in Example 2.11 from the course notes, where heat transfer is modeled in analog to electrical current flow through resistors.

Under these assumptions, the total heat transfer rate is expressed as

\begin{equation}
\dot{Q} = \frac{T_b - T_{\infty}}{R_{\text{tot}}}
\end{equation}

where $T_b$ is the duct base temperature, $T_{\infty}$ is the temperature of the fluid, and $R_{\text{tot}}$ is the total thermal resistance of the fin bank.

The modeling procedure follows a structured four-step process (simplified from the five-step process used in class by parallel resistances):

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Determine finned and unfinned surface areas
    \item Compute fin efficiency
    \item Construct the thermal resistance network
    \item Solve for heat transfer rate and system performance
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Part 2 - Cooling System}
\subsubsection{Introduction to Part 2}

The second part of this project focused on modifying our model to account for the cooling system, i.e. the air flow through the duct from a cooling fan. This proved to be a challenge, since there are many parts of the problem that are a function of the convective heat transfer coefficient $h$. Ultimately, we decided to create a system that can iteratively solve for this coefficient, given a certain fin thermal resistance and using the conservation of energy. To simplify the calculation of $h$, we treated the space between the fins as an enclosed duct and calculated the Reynolds number for this space. The diagram for this surrogate duct can be found in Figure XXX. %%FIXME

Part 2 of the project was the most computationally complex by far, due to computing hydraulic diameter and Reynolds number for the flow between fins, the bulk temperature and mean air velocity in the duct, the convective heat transfer coefficient of the flow between fins, and the fan power required to cool the system.

\subsubsection{Cooling Flow Model with Convective Heat Transfer}

In Part 2, the fin-bank model is extended to include forced convection through the duct. Unlike Part 1, the convection coefficient $h$ is no longer prescribed but instead determined by modeling the fluid flow. Because of this, the heat transfer of the system can be determined from two sides: the conduction/convection of the fins and the conservation of energy. This introduces a coupling of the two halves that can be solved iteratively for an $h$ that satisfies both.

In other words, $\dot{Q}$ (the heat that can be dissipated by the system) can be determined as described in Part 1 above. $\dot{Q}$ can also be determined by applying the conservation of energy:

\begin{equation}
\dot{Q} = \dot{m}c_p(T_{m,e}-T_{m,i})
\end{equation} 

where $T_{m,e}$ and $T_{m,i}$ are the exit and inlet temperatures of the duct, respectively; $\dot{m}$ is the mass flow rate through the duct; and $c_p$ is the specific heat of the air.

By setting these values equal to each other with $h$ as an independent variable, an algebraic loop is created that can be solved by iteration. This produces a value for $h$ and $T_{m,e}$.

However, we decided to break the algebraic loop at a slightly different point so that we could express $h$ and $T_{m,e}$ as function of $\dot{Q}_{CPU}$. This is because with the method discussed above, the $\dot{Q}$ value drops out of the equation and thus is not an independent variable. Since the problem statement requires setting $\dot{Q}_{CPU}$, this modification was necessary.

To accomplish this change, we decided to solve both sides for $T_{bulk}$, the free-stream average temperature in the duct. Note that in Part 1, $T_{\infty} = T_{bulk}$. Setting the bulk temperature for each half equal allows us to set $\dot{Q}_{CPU}$ and solve for the desired variables. This procedure is outlined in more detail in the Calculations section below.

We built a simple iterative solver in MATLAB which performs a search incrementally adjusting $h$ until the sign of $f(h)$ changes, followed by successive reductions of the step size to refine the solution. This process continues until the difference between the two temperature estimates falls below a prescribed tolerance.

At each iteration, the Reynolds number, velocity, mass flow rate, and temperatures are updated, allowing for a complete description of the system and plotting parameters.

\subsubsection{Surrogate Duct and Flow Assumptions}

To make these calculations possible, the flow between fins is modeled as a series of identical rectangular ducts (referred to as surrogate ducts), formed by the spaces between adjacent fins. This simplifies the Reynolds number calculation since these spaces are rectangular. This is a reasonable assumption because most of the convective heat transfer occurs within these spaces. The flow is also assumed to be turbulent and fully developed, since this also greatly simplifies the calculation. These assumptions will be evaluated in a later section.

Additionally, before calculations could start, air properties had to be determined. To do so, we assumed a $T_{bulk}$ of $35^\circ C$, which is the average of the inlet temperature $T_{m,i}$ and the maximum exit temperature $T_{m,e}$.

\begin{equation}
T_{bulk} \approx \frac{T_{m,i} + T_{m,e}}{2}.
\end{equation}

This was done to select $\rho$ (density, $\tfrac{kg}{m^3}$), $c_p$ (specific heat $\tfrac{J}{kg \cdot K}$), $k$ (thermal conductivity, $\tfrac{W}{m\cdot K})$, $\nu$ (kinematic viscosity, $\tfrac{m^2}{s}$), and $Pr$ (Prandtl number). However, after simulation, our $T_{m,e}$ values were much closer to $40^\circ C$ (see tables in appendix), so our initial assumptions were modified to fit this result ($T_{bulk} \approx 30^\circ C$).

\subsubsection{Conduction Considerations}

This part of the problem also included the addition of thermal conduction through a thermal paste layer and the walls of the duct. This is reflected in the thermal resistance diagram in the calculations section below. For the thermal paste, we selected ``\href{https://www.tomshardware.com/best-picks/best-thermal-paste}{ID-Cooling Frost X45}'', which advertises a thermal conductivity of 15.2 $\tfrac{W}{m\cdot K}$. We know that 

\begin{equation}
h_c = \frac{k}{t}
\end{equation}

so an application of this paste with a thickness of $t \approx 0.5 mm$ results in a thermal contact coefficient of $30,000\ \tfrac{W}{m^2\cdot K}$. This is on the thicker end of applications, but trying with different thicknesses did not vary results much under a certain threshold.

Additionally, thermal resistance from conduction was included in the calculation. We decided to use copper for our duct due to its high thermal conductivity. Varying this parameter in Part 3 confirms this decision.

\subsection{Assumptions}

Our simplifying assumptions can be seen below:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Assume all heat from CPUs is dissipated through the fin bank (no heat loss to the external environment). This greatly reduced the amount of work and considerations needed for later calculations. 

\item Assume 1-D heat transfer for the conduction and convection sections (perpendicular to the duct wall and perpendicular to the fins). This is a typical assumption for fin bank analysis. 

\item Assume steady state conduction and fin convention. These are typical assumptions for fin bank analysis.

\item Assume that, for a given side, there is a space between the first fin and the wall, and a space between the last fin and the first fin on the adjacent sides. This prevents odd behavior between touching fins and keeps the surrogate ducts identical.

\item Assume each fin has an identical profile. This reduces work by allowing us to calculate the heat transfer through one fin and simply scale by the number of fins.

\item Assume perfectly caloric air. This helps us in our calculations of the volume flow rate of the air.

\item Assume conservation of mass. This allows us to calculate the mean velocity of the air.

\item Assume $T_{bulk} = 30^\circ C$. This allows us to compute many calculations that would be unsolvable otherwise, as well as determine certain values from a properties table. Why we chose this value is explained above. 

\item Assume turbulent airflow. This is to simplify several calculations, and is what we would expect from this type of duct and fin bank.

\item Assume thin fins, no heat transfer out of the sides of the fins, and 1D heat transfer. This is a common fin bank analysis technique.

\item Assume changes in kinetic and potential energy are negligible. This is necessary to simplify the First Law of Thermodynamics, and is a reasonable assumption for the convection through the fins since there is not a large change in either. %%FIXME
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Fin Efficiency}

Single fin efficiency is needed for resistance calculations. With the thin fin assumption, the fin efficiency can be found with the following equations:

\begin{equation}
m = \sqrt{\frac{2h}{kt}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
L_c = L + \frac{t}{2}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\eta_{\text{fin}} = \frac{\tanh(mL_c)}{mL_c}
\end{equation}

For Part 1, this results in a single fin efficiency of $\eta_{\text{fin}} =  0.9412$. This formulation accounts for conduction along the fin and convection from its surface, consistent with standard fin analysis derived from an energy balance on a differential element.

\subsubsection{Fin Geometry and Surface Areas}

From the above geometric constraints, the area of the components can be calculated. This is necessary, since the total heat transfer area consists of contributions from both the fins and the unfinned duct surface. These are defined as base area

\begin{equation}
A_b = wt = 0.0003\ m^2,
\end{equation}

single fin area

\begin{equation}
A_{\text{fin}} = 2wL_c = 0.0078\ m^2,
\end{equation}

total fin area

\begin{equation}
A_{\text{fins}} = N A_{\text{fin}} = 0.7488\ m^2,
\end{equation}

and wet surface area with no fins

\begin{equation}
A_{\text{unfin}} = A_{\text{nofins}} - N A_b = 0.09\ m^2.
\end{equation}

Note that the total area could be computed to calculate the total efficiency, but our team found it more straightforward to calculate parallel thermal resistances for the fins and unfinned regions. Note that $N$ is the total number of fins and and $L_c$ is a corrected fin length so that the tip of the fin is included in the side length for 1D heat transfer.

\subsubsection{Thermal Resistance Network}

With the above calculations complete, the thermal resistance of the network can be computed. Figure 3 gives a representation of the entire project in thermal resistances.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{Thermal Resistance Diagram.jpg}
    \caption{Thermal Resistance Diagram}
\end{figure}

Assumptions associated with this model can be found in the assumptions section of the introduction. Note that for Part 1, $R_{paste}$ and $R_{cond}$ are ignored to match Example 2.11. 

The finned surfaces and the unfinned duct surface work in parallel. Using the thermal resistance analogy, the convection thermal resistances of the finned and unfinned areas respectively are

\begin{equation}
R_{\text{fins}} = \frac{1}{h A_{\text{fins}} \eta_{\text{fin}}} = 0.0284\ \frac{K}{W}
\end{equation}

and

\begin{equation}
R_{\text{unfin}} = \frac{1}{h A_{\text{unfin}}} = 0.3268\ \frac{K}{W}.
\end{equation}

Calculating the total resistance of the parallel network, we obtain

\begin{equation}
R_{\text{fin bank}} = \left( \frac{1}{R_{\text{fins}}} + \frac{1}{R_{\text{unfin}}} \right)^{-1} =  0.0261\ \frac{K}{W}. 
\end{equation}

This formulation reflects the physical reality that heat simultaneously transfers through both regions, analogous to current splitting across parallel resistors.

\subsubsection{Heat Transfer Rate and CPU Power}

Using the total resistance and the analogous $I = \tfrac{V}{R}$ equation, the total heat transfer from the fin bank is

\begin{equation}
\dot{Q}_{\text{tot}} = \frac{T_b - T_{\infty}}{R_{\text{tot}}} = 1914.9\ W.
\end{equation}

The allowable heat dissipation per CPU (or transistor in Example 2.11) is then

\begin{equation}
\dot{Q}_{\text{CPU}} = \frac{\dot{Q}_{\text{tot}}}{N_{\text{CPU}}} = 53.1908\ W
\end{equation}

This value matches the in-class calculation. As mentioned, later parts of the project will replace $T_{\infty}$ with $T_{bulk }$.

\subsubsection{Airflow Requirements (Energy Balance)}

To determine the required airflow, a steady-flow energy balance (First Law of Thermodynamics) is applied to the air passing through the duct:

\begin{equation}
\dot{Q}_{\text{tot}} = \dot{m}_{\text{air}} c_p \Delta T_{\text{air}}
\end{equation}

Solving for the mass flow rate gives

\begin{equation}
\dot{m}_{\text{air}} = \frac{\dot{Q}_{\text{tot}}}{c_p \Delta T_{\text{air}}} = 0.1903\ \frac{kg}{s}.
\end{equation}

This value, when divided by the density of the air ($\rho = 1.14\ \tfrac{kg}{m^3}$) results in the desired volume flow rate of $0.167\ \tfrac{m^3}{s}$. We did not get this far in class, but the mass flow rate matches our work. Note that the relation used above assumes constant specific heat and negligible kinetic and potential energy changes, as listed in the assumptions section.


\subsubsection{Verification of Part 1 Model}

Since the values above match the in-class calculations, we can conclude that given our assumptions, our model is accurate. We will use this model in parts 2 and 3 to add more facets to the design.

\subsection{Part 2 - Cooling System}

This part of the project adds cooling flow to calculate an $h$ value and the additional thermal resistances shown in Figure 3. This calculation assumes that $P_{CPU}$ (the total CPU power in Watts) is known and uses it to find the necessary fan power $P_{fan}$ (total fan power in Watts). To accomplish this, an $h$ value must be determined using the iterative solving method above. Thus, the calculations below determine $T_{bulk}$ from both the method discussed in Part 1 and the conservation of energy. Since these are both functions of $h$, $h$ can be iterated to find the value where they are equal. Thus, the equations below will seek to determine $T_{bulk}$ as a function of $h$.

\subsubsection{Nusselt Number and Convection Coefficient}

For the reasons listed in the assumptions section, we assume turbulent flow. Therefore, the following relation can be used for the Nusselt number (note that $Re_D$ is used to signify the Reynolds number for the duct):

\begin{equation}
Nu = 0.023 Re_D^{0.8} Pr^{0.4}.
\end{equation}

The convection heat transfer coefficient is then

\begin{equation}
h = \frac{Nu \, k_{\text{air}}}{D_h}
\end{equation}

where $k_{air}$ is the thermal conductivity of the air and $D_h$ is the hydraulic diameter of the surrogate duct in meters. Combining these relations and solving for the Reynolds number yields the following relationship: 

\begin{equation}
Re_D = \left(\frac{hD_h}{0.023 k_{air} Pr^{0.4}}\right)^{\frac{1}{0.8}}
\label{eq:re}
\end{equation}

This means that we have the Reynolds number for the surrogate duct as a function of $h$.

\subsubsection{Reynolds Number and Mean Velocity}

The Reynolds number for the surrogate duct is defined as

\begin{equation}
Re_D = \frac{u_m D_h}{\nu} %%FIXME hydraulic diameter
\label{eq:red}
\end{equation}

where $u_m$ is the mean velocity (in $\tfrac{m}{s}$), $D_h$ is the hydraulic diameter (in $m$), and $\nu$ is the kinematic viscosity from above. Note that hydraulic diameter for a rectangular duct can be calculated using

\begin{equation}
D_h = \frac{2ab}{a+b}
\end{equation}

where $a$ and $b$ are the side lengths of the duct.  This calculation is performed for the surrogate duct dimensions of $L$ and $s$ (see Figure 4 and Figure 2). For the dimensions in Part 1, we find $D_h = 0.0055\ m$.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{Surrogate Duct Diagram.JPG}
    \caption{Surrogate "Duct" Diagram}
\end{figure}

Rearranging (\ref{eq:red}) gives the mean velocity:

\begin{equation}
u_m = \frac{Re_D \nu}{D_h}.
\label{eq:um}
\end{equation}

In other words, now we have the mean velocity of the stream in terms of $h$. 

\subsubsection{Mass Flow Rate (Conservation of Mass)}
Continuing to work upwards towards $T_{bulk}$, we apply conservation of mass to find

\begin{equation}
\dot{m}_{\text{air}} = \rho A_c u_m.
\label{eq:m}
\end{equation}

where $A_c$ is the cross-sectional flow area (in $m^2$) and $\rho$ is the air density from above. This means we have $\dot{m}_{\text{air}}$ in terms of $h$

\subsubsection{Energy Balance on the Air (First Law)}
Finally, we apply the First Law of Thermodynamics to the control volume of air flowing through the duct:

\begin{equation}
\dot{Q}_{\text{tot}} = \dot{m}_{\text{air}} c_p (T_{m,e} - T_{m,i})
\end{equation}

where $c_p$ is the specific heat from above and $T_{m,i}$ is the inlet temperature, set to $20^\circ C$. Rearranging this gives the mean exit air temperature (now in terms of $h$):

\begin{equation}
T_{m,e} = T_{m,i} + \frac{\dot{Q}_{\text{tot}}}{\dot{m}_{\text{air}} c_p}
\label{eq:tme}
\end{equation}

Since it is assumed that

\begin{equation}
T_{bulk} = \frac{T_{m,i} + T_{m,e}}{2}, 
\end{equation}

(\ref{eq:tme}) can be rearranged to

\begin{equation}
T_{bulk} = \frac{\frac{\dot{Q}_{tot}}{\dot{m}_{air}c_p} + 2 T_{m,i}}{2};
\end{equation}

We can now equate this value (which is a function of $h$) to the $T_{bulk}$  found by using the procedure in Step 1. Again, this allows us to iterate and find $h$.

\subsubsection{Thermal Resistance with Conduction and Contact Effects}

Part 1 neglects the thermal resistance of the paste and the conduction through the wall. These values must be computed using the $h_c$ calculated in the introduction.

\begin{equation}
R_{\text{paste}} = \frac{1}{h_c A_{\text{CPUs}}}.
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
R_{\text{cond}} = \frac{t_{\text{duct}}}{k_{\text{duct}} A_{\text{walls}}}.
\end{equation}

In the above equations, $A_{\text{CPUs}}$ is the total surface area of all of the CPUs (in $m^2$), $A_{\text{walls}}$ is the total surface area of the walls (in $m^2$), and $k_{duct}$ is the thermal conductivity of the duct. Per the table of compliance below, we used copper with $k_{duct} = 392 \tfrac{W}{m \cdot K}$. See the appendices for the table of $k$ values for various materials.

The above thermal resistances are combined in series with the fin bank resistance to obtain the total thermal resistance:

\begin{equation}
R_{\text{total, system}} = R_{\text{paste}} + R_{\text{cond}} + R_{\text{fin bank}} =  0.0322\ \frac{K}{W}.
\end{equation}

Note that $R_{\text{fin bank}}$ is still a function of $h$.

\subsubsection{Convection/Conduction $T_{bulk}$ Calculation}

Using the new resistance above and the method from Part 1, we can determine $\dot{Q}$ for the fin bank:

\begin{equation}
\dot{Q} = \frac{90^\circ C - T_{bulk}}{R_{\text{total, system}}}
\end{equation}

See Figure 3---$90^\circ C$ is the maximum CPU temperature. Rearranging this to solve for $T_{bulk}$, we find that

\begin{equation}
T_{bulk} = 90^\circ C - \dot{Q}R_{total, system}
\end{equation}

Since this is a function of $h$, we can finally set it equal to the $T_{bulk}$ found above and iteratively solve for $h$. See \texttt{part2.m}, \texttt{findh2.m}, and \texttt{tinf.m} in the appendix for the inner workings of the iterative solver, also discussed broadly in the introduction.

\subsubsection{Fan Performance and Power Requirement}

Once the thermal convection coefficient is determined ($40.3044\ \tfrac{W}{m^2\cdot K}$ for the Part 1 setup), it can be substituted back into (\ref{eq:re}) to find $Re_D$. Part 1 geometry yields $Re_D = 1878.5$---note that this value indicates laminar flow since it is less than about 2300. This can be substituted into (\ref{eq:um}) to find $u_m$ (about $5.6688\ \tfrac{m}{s}$) and this result can then be inserted into (\ref{eq:m}) to find mass flow rate ($\dot{m}_{air}$).

Once this is determined, the fan power can be determined from the problem statement document

\begin{equation}
Q_{\text{fan}} = \frac{\dot{m}_{\text{air}}}{\rho}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
P_{\text{fan}} = 10{,}000 \left(\frac{Q_{\text{fan}}}{100{,}000}\right)^3.
\end{equation}

For Part 1 geometry (and equivalent CPU power), the fan power is $2.1819\ W$, which is well under the maximum $1000\ W$ from the problem statement. This yields a performance score of 20.3402 (see below for score calculation).

\subsubsection{Verification of Part 2 Model}

We do not have an example problem to verify our calculations against, but in comparison with real world values, our numbers make sense. The fan power and velocity are reasonable, and our resulting $h$ value is within the range of expected values.

\subsection{Part 3 - Performance Analysis}

\subsubsection{Performance Metric}

The performance of the system is quantified as

\begin{equation}
\text{Performance} = \frac{CPU_{\text{perf, total}}}{P_{\text{tot}}}
\end{equation}

where the total power is given by

\begin{equation}
P_{\text{tot}} = P_{\text{CPUs}} + P_{\text{fan}}.
\end{equation}


The CPU performance score is related to the power of the CPUs using the provided relation

\begin{equation}
CPU_{\text{perf}} = 39000\left(1 - e^{-0.02P_{CPUs}}\right)
\label{eq:ref}
\end{equation}

which can be inverted to determine the required CPU power for a specified performance level. 
(\ref{eq:ref}) seems like it should probably use single CPU power, since CPU performance is very high even with low CPU power, but for our project, we have followed the the problem statement document and used total CPU power. This may result in lower overall performance scores.

The function \texttt{perf.m} (see appendix) is used to evaluate this system performance score for a given fin geometry and operating condition by solving the coupled models described in detail above. It internally determines the convection coefficient through the iterative procedure, computes the required airflow and fan power, and returns the ratio of CPU performance to total system power.

\subsubsection{Parametric Analysis}

For Part 3, a parametric study is conducted by varying one design parameter at a time while holding all others constant at the baseline geometry. For each configuration, the coupled model from Parts 1 and 2 is solved to determine the required mass flow, convection coefficient, and fan power.

The following parameters are evaluated:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fin length ($L$)} 
    \item \textbf{Number of fins ($N$)} 
    \item \textbf{Fin thickness ($t$)} 
    \item \textbf{Material conductivity ($k$)}
\end{itemize}

For each case, the performance metric is computed as described above and plotted as a function of the varied parameter.

\subsubsection{Two-Parameter Analysis}

In addition to single-parameter sweeps, a two-dimensional analysis is performed by simultaneously varying the number of fins and the CPU power. A surface plot is generated to illustrate the dependence of system performance on these variables:

\begin{equation}
\text{Performance} = f(N, P_{CPU})
\end{equation}

This analysis provides insight into how optimal fin design varies with operating conditions.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Variation of Material (Conductivity)}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{Performance_Material.png}
    \caption{Performance vs. Duct Material}
\end{figure}

The above graph illustrates a significant performance improvement moving from a poor material choice (such as steel with a low k-coefficient) to better materials (such as copper or better materials). The increase from a poor material to a better material grants significant gains, but once the material is already a good choice, the increase from that good material to a better material means that the fin efficiency is already near-ideal, so there is minimal further benefit.

\subsection{Variation of Fin Count}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{Performance_Count.png}
    \caption{Performance vs. Fin Count}
\end{figure}

It can be observed that there is a strong increase in performance when initially increasing the fin count N, as is reasonably expected because more fins corresponds to more surface area and thus better cooling; however, an increase in fin count also implies less spacing between fins, smaller hydraulic diameter $D_h$ and higher flow resistance. Because of these drawbacks, the benefits gained from better cooling are eventually canceled out by the practical penalty implied by a finite area; therefore, the curve eventually flattening out is expected. For design, the optimal region—similarly to that of the prior performance versus fin length relationship—is that at which the curve flattens at around the 50 fan point.

\subsection{Variation of Fin Thickness}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{Performance_Thickness.png}
    \caption{Performance vs. Fin Thickness}
\end{figure}

The graph of performance versus fin thickness demonstrates a clear relationship in which there is a very sharp increase in performance once the fins are not incredibly thin, as is intuitive because improving the conduction correlates with a higher fin efficiency. Following this increase, however, is a clear, flat plateau in which increasing fin thickness provides no further benefit. This makes sense because reducing the spacing between fins significantly decreases airflow, thereby canceling out the benefit offered by increased conduction. Thus, the optimal region for design is at the point of diminishing returns, corresponding to a fin thickness of approximately 0.2 m.

\subsection{Variation of Fin Length}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{Performance_Length.png}
    \caption{Performance vs. Fin Length}
\end{figure}

As can be seen from the graph of performance versus fin length L, there is a rapid increase in performance at a small L, then followed by a clear plateau. This is intuitively reasonable because increasing L increases the effective surface area, thereby improving the heat transfer; however, fin efficiency drops along the length as temperature decays. The extra area added, therefore, becomes thermally inactive, leading to a lower fin efficiency. Thus, for design, the optimal region is that at which the curve flattens, at around the 0.02 m point.

\subsection{Variation of CPU Power and Number of Fins}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{Performance_PCPU_N.png}
    \caption{Performance vs. Single $P_{PCU}$ and Total Number of Fins}
\end{figure}

The above surface plot illustrates the relationship between system performance, CPU power, and fin count. Increasing the number of fins generally improves performance due to the added heat transfer area, while increasing CPU power initially increases performance due to higher computational output. However, at higher power levels, the required cooling becomes more demanding, and the fan power increases rapidly due to its nonlinear dependence on airflow. As a result, performance gains begin to diminish, especially at high CPU power and high fin counts, where the cooling system becomes the limiting factor. This demonstrates that while both increasing fin count and CPU power can improve performance, their combined effects are constrained by the efficiency and power requirements of the cooling system. 

\subsection{Evaluation of Assumptions From Part 1 and 2 Based on Results}

Looking back at our assumptions, most were either correct or incredibly useful in simplifying our calculations. For example, the typical fin bank assumptions (1-D heat transfer, thin fins, steady state, etc.) reduced a great deal of work in this project. Additionally, assumptions such as perfectly caloric air, conservation of mass, and negligible changes in kinetic and potential energy allowed us to perform calculations that would otherwise be either very difficult or unsolvable.

However, not all of our assumptions were correct. Specifically, we assumed that the airflow would be turbulent, as this would be expected from this type of duct and fin bank. During our Part 1 calculations, however, we found that the Reynold's number for our design was 1,878.5. While this isn't too far off from the requirement for turbulent airflow (roughly 2,300), it is still low enough to be classified as laminar flow, which is not what we expected. That said, it is entirely possible that the fan will introduce enough force to make the flow turbulent. 

\section{Conclusions}

After analyzing our results, we can conclude that while there are numerous variables that affect the overall performance of the duct system, a few in particular affect the performance more than others. Fin count and fin length specifically seem to have a greater variation of performance based on their value, especially when compared to duct material and fin thickness. This makes sense, as fin count and length affect the fin surface area significantly more then fin thickness and duct material. The surface area than drastically changes how much heat is able to be transferred.

Additionally, we noted that regardless of how you change the variables, there is an ultimate ceiling the overall performance can reach. Notice that in the plots for each variable, each one maxes out around 11.36. This tells us that although you can absolutely get worse performance by changing variables, you can only make the performance so good until you reach diminishing returns.

m = sqrt((2 * h) / (k * t));         %constant for calculating efficiency

eta_fin = tanh(m * L_c) / (m * L_c); %single fin efficiency

%% Calculate Areas
A_b = w * t;                         %area of single fin base, meters^2
A_fin = 2 * w * L_c;                 %area of single fin, meters^2
A_fins = N * A_fin;                  %area of all of the fins, meters^2
A_nofins = L_SIDE * L_DUCT * 4;      %area with fins removed, meters^2
A_unfin = A_nofins - N * A_b;        %area of fin bank with no fins, meters^2
A_tot = A_fins + A_unfin;            %total area of fin bank, meters^2
%eta_tot = 1 - ((A_fins) / (A_tot)) * (1 - eta_fin); %unused total efficiency

%% Calculate Fin Convection Resistance
%R_tot = 1 / (h * A_tot * eta_tot) 
R_fins = 1 / (h * A_fins * eta_fin); %fins convection thermal resistance, K/W
R_unfin = 1 / (h * A_unfin);         %unfin convection thermal resistance, K/W
R_tot = 1/(1/R_fins+1/R_unfin);      %total resistance - parallel resistors, K/W

Q_dot_tran = Q_dot_tot/N_CPU         %allowed power per transistor

%% b) Determine Required Volume Flow Rate of Air
%assume perfectly caloric
m_dot_air = Q_dot_tot/(c_p*dT_air)   %minimum volume flow rate, kg/s

\begin{verbatim}
function [h, Re_D, T_me] = findh2(P_CPUs, h_c, t_DUCT, A_c, ...
    D_h, T_mi, c_p, k_air, k_duct, A_c_walls, A_c_CPUs, N, ...
    t, L, L_SIDE, L_DUCT, w, rho, nu, Pr)
%convection heat transfer coefficient, W/(m^2*K) CHECK UNITS
    it = 1;
    precision = 0.00001;
    h = 1;
    while it >= precision
        [new, Re_D, T_me] = tinf(P_CPUs, h, h_c, t_DUCT, ...
            A_c, D_h, T_mi, c_p, k_air, k_duct, A_c_walls, ...
            A_c_CPUs, N, t, L, L_SIDE, L_DUCT, w, rho, nu, Pr);
        while new >= 0
            [new, Re_D, T_me] = tinf(P_CPUs, h, h_c, t_DUCT, ...
                A_c, D_h, T_mi, c_p, k_air, k_duct, ...
                A_c_walls, A_c_CPUs, N, t, L, L_SIDE, ...
                L_DUCT, w, rho, nu, Pr);
            h = h + it;
        end
        h = h - 2 * it;
        it = it/10;
    end
end

    s = (L_SIDE-L-N/4*t)/(N/4+1);        %Spacing between fins, meters
    A_c = L_SIDE^2 - L*t*N;              %Cross sectional area, meters^2
    D_h = (2*L*s)/(L+s); %Hydraulic diameter of surragate duct, meters
    [h, Re_D, T_me] = findh2(P_CPUs, h_c, t_DUCT, A_c, D_h, ...
        T_mi, c_p, k_air, k_duct, A_c_walls, A_c_CPUs, N, ...
        t, L, L_SIDE, L_DUCT, w, rho, nu, Pr)

    %% Calculate Mean Velocity
    %assume conservation of mass to use:
    %Reynolds number for surrogate duct:
    u_m = Re_D * nu / D_h
    
    %% Calculate mass flow rate
    %given T_me = T_mi + Q_dot/(m_dot * c_p)
    m_dot_air = rho * A_c * u_m; %kg/s %Q_dot_tot/(c_p*(T_me-T_mi))
    %commented out is the necessary m_dot - requires iterative solution
    
    %Q_fan conversion
    Q_fan = (m_dot_air / rho) * 60 * 1000;   %In L/min
    
    P_fan = ((Q_fan/100000)^3)*10000;

    perf_out = CPU_perf/(P_CPUs+P_fan);
end
\end{verbatim}
\subsubsection{tinf.m}
\begin{verbatim}
function [diff, Re_D, T_me] = tinf(P_CPUs, h, h_c, t_DUCT, ...
    A_c, D_h, T_mi, c_p, k_air, k_duct, A_c_walls, A_c_CPUs, ...
    N, t, L, L_SIDE, L_DUCT, w, rho, nu, Pr)
    Q_dot_tot = P_CPUs;
    
    %% Calculate Reynolds Number
    %Nusselt Number Eqn:
    Re_D = ((h*D_h)/(k_air*0.023*Pr^0.4))^(1/0.8); %Reynolds number for the duct
    
    %% Calculate Mean Velocity
    %assume conservation of mass to use:
    %Reynolds number for surrogate duct:
    u_m = Re_D * nu / D_h;
    
    %% Calculate mass flow rate
    %given T_me = T_mi + Q_dot/(m_dot * c_p)
    m_dot_air = rho * A_c * u_m; %kg/s %Q_dot_tot/(c_p*(T_me-T_mi))
    %commented out is the necessary m_dot - requires iterative solution
    
    T_inf_air = (Q_dot_tot/(m_dot_air*c_p) + 2 * T_mi)/2;
    
    %% Calculate Thermal Resistances
    R_paste = 1/(h_c * A_c_CPUs);            
    R_cond = t_DUCT/(k_duct * A_c_walls);
    
    %% Calculate Fin Efficiency
    m = sqrt((2 * h) / (k_duct * t));    %constant for calculating efficiency
    L_c = L + t / 2;                     %corrected fin length, meters
    eta_fin = tanh(m * L_c) / (m * L_c); %single fin efficiency
    
    %% Calculate Areas
    A_b = w * t;                         %area of single fin base, meters^2
    A_fin = 2 * w * L_c;                 %area of single fin, meters^2
    A_fins = N * A_fin;                  %area of all of the fins, meters^2
    A_nofins = L_SIDE * L_DUCT * 4;      %area with fins removed, meters^2
    A_unfin = A_nofins - N * A_b; %area of fin bank with no fins, meters^2
    A_tot = A_fins + A_unfin;            %total area of fin bank, meters^2
    %eta_tot = 1 - ((A_fins) / (A_tot)) * (1 - eta_fin);
    %unused total efficiency
    
    %% Calculate Fin Convection Resistance
    %R_tot = 1 / (h * A_tot * eta_tot) 
    %fins convection thermal resistance, K/W
    R_fins = 1 / (h * A_fins * eta_fin);
    R_unfin = 1 / (h * A_unfin); %unfin convection thermal resistance, K/W
    %total resistance - parallel fin and unfin resistors, K/W
    R_tot = 1/(1/R_fins+1/R_unfin);
    
    T_inf_fin = 90 - Q_dot_tot * (R_paste + R_cond + R_tot);
    
    diff = T_inf_air - T_inf_fin;

    T_me = Q_dot_tot/(m_dot_air * c_p) +T_mi;
end
\end{verbatim}
